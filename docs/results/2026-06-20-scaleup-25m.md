# 25M Scale-Up: Does the Ratchet Track FP32?

## Question

At the tiny 0.5M config the ratchet beat FP32 (quinary ~1.83 vs FP32 ~1.93). Small
quantized models often punch above their weight, then fall behind as size grows. Does the
ratchet's advantage *persist* one model-size jump up (~25M matrix params)?

## Protocol

`configs/scaleup_25m.toml`: block_size 256, n_layer 8, n_head 8, n_embd 512, dropout 0.1,
3,000 steps, seeds 1337 and 1338. Three matched arms — quinary (`--codes 5`), septenary
(`--codes 7`), and FP32 (`--weight-mode fp32`) — trained in parallel across two RTX 3090s.
Char-level Tiny Shakespeare. `lat audit` reported zero violations.

Because the model has ~25M parameters and the corpus is ~1MB, all arms overfit, so the fair
comparison is **best validation loss over the trajectory**, not the step-3000 value.

## Results (means over two seeds)

| Arm | Best val | Best-val step | Final val | Final saturation | Cumulative moves |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| FP32 | 1.538 | ~1700 | 1.725 | — | 0 |
| Septenary (2.81-bit) | 1.534 | ~2850 | 1.542 | 11% | ~223k |
| Quinary (2.32-bit) | 1.692 | 3000 | 1.692 | 17% | ~184k |

(FP32 per seed: best 1.538 @1900 / 1.538 @1500. Septenary: 1.531 @2800 / 1.537 @2900.
Quinary: 1.710 / 1.674, both still at their step-3000 minimum.)

## Findings

1. **The plain "ratchet beats FP32" did not survive scaling.** Quinary clearly trails FP32
   at 25M (1.692 vs 1.538), reversing its tiny-scale edge. Its advantage was partly a
   small-model effect.

2. **Septenary ties FP32 — and is more robust.** Septenary's best val (1.534) matches FP32
   (1.538) within noise, reached without meaningful overfitting, while FP32 bottomed at
   ~step 1700 then degraded to 1.725. A 2.81-bit, master-weight-free, optimizer-state-free
   model matching full-precision AdamW at 25M is a strong result; the discreteness acts as a
   powerful regularizer.

3. **Saturation dropped sharply with scale.** Quinary 58% (tiny) -> 17% (25M); septenary
   47% -> 11%. Boundary pinning was a small-model artifact, not an intrinsic scaling
   problem — the most encouraging signal for scaling further.

## Caveat

At 3,000 steps the comparison is unfair *to the ratchet*: FP32 had already overfit and
turned up, while both ratchet arms were still descending (quinary's best equals its final
step). The ratchet regularizes hard enough that it had not finished converging, so these
numbers slightly undersell it. Longer training or more data is needed to let the ratchet
arms plateau against FP32 before drawing a firm verdict.
